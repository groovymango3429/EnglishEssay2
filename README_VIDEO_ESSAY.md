# Video Essay Script - Usage Guide

## Overview
This repository now contains a complete video essay script (`video_essay_script.md`) for the Final Reflection assignment.

## What's Included

The script meets all rubric requirements:

### ✅ Thesis Structure (What/How/So-What)
- **What:** Effective communication requires intentional design
- **How:** Discovered through analyzing rhetoric and applying feedback loops
- **So What:** Changes approach to all future persuasion and communication

### ✅ Content Requirements
- **Length:** ~802 speaking words (5 minutes at 160 words per minute)
- **Honest Reflection:** Addresses real learning, not performative feedback
- **Major Assignments Referenced:** Rhetorical Analysis and Inquiry Dialogue
- **Habits Identified:** Pause before making claims, catch intellectual dishonesty
- **Future Applications:** Social media literacy, job interviews, critical thinking
- **Ongoing Questions:** Visual rhetoric creation (beyond analysis)

### ✅ Video Essay Format
- Visual cues throughout for B-roll footage
- Talking head segments clearly marked
- Professional but conversational tone
- Clear section timing guidelines

## How to Use This Script

### Recording Preparation
1. Read through the entire script 2-3 times to familiarize yourself
2. Gather visual materials mentioned:
   - Screenshots from your rhetorical analysis assignment
   - Examples of ethos/pathos/logos you analyzed
   - Your inquiry dialogue (original and revised questions)
   - Feedback comments you received
   - Examples of contexts (social media, news articles, etc.)

### Recording Tips
- **Natural Delivery:** Speak conversationally, not like reading
- **Pacing:** 160 words per minute is comfortable; adjust as needed
- **Pauses:** Use strategic pauses for emphasis (noted in script)
- **Visual Integration:** Show evidence while discussing it
- **Eye Contact:** Look at camera during thesis and key points
- **B-Roll:** Use visuals during specific examples to enhance the "show and tell" aspect

### Video Editing
- Start with title card featuring your name
- Alternate between talking head and visual examples
- Use text overlays sparingly for emphasis (thesis, key questions)
- End with closing text as specified
- Ensure video experience is different from audio alone
